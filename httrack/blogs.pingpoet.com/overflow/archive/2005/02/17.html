A National ID Bill Masquerading as Immigration Reform

HR 418 is a scary bill that was recently passed in the house. The bill's summary states:

To establish and rapidly implement regulations for State driver's license and identification document security standards, to prevent terrorists from abusing the asylum laws of the United States, to unify terrorism-related grounds for inadmissibility and removal, and to ensure expeditious construction of the San Diego border fence.

I'm not sure how productive "putting up a fence" (seriously) between the U.S. and Mexico near San Diego is, but Congressman Ron Paul recently made some comments before the US House of Representatives that I found interesting. I'll quote some highlights for you below:

I rise in strong opposition to HR 418, the REAL ID Act. This bill purports to make us safer from terrorists who may sneak into the United States, and from other illegal immigrants. While I agree that these issues are of vital importance, this bill will do very little to make us more secure. It will not address our real vulnerabilities. It will, however, make us much less free. In reality, this bill is a Trojan horse. It pretends to offer desperately needed border control in order to stampede Americans into sacrificing what is uniquely American: our constitutionally protected liberty.

Although Congress funded an additional 2,000 border guards last year, the administration has announced that it will only ask for an additional 210 guards. Why are we not pursuing these avenues as a way of safeguarding our country? Why are we punishing Americans by taking away their freedoms instead of making life more difficult for those who would enter our country illegally?

He goes onto make the point that the bill gives authority to the Secretary of Homeland Security to unilaterally expand the required information on driverâ€™s licenses as he sees fit. Requirements potentially including such biometric information as retina scans, finger prints, DNA information, and even Radio Frequency Identification (RFID) radio tracking technology. Technology such as RFID means that government officials, or anyone else with the proper means, can learn whatever considerable information the government knows about you without you asking anyone, let alone you.

Considering congress just gave a D+ grade on the overall security of computer systems inside the largest U.S. government agencies, as noted in this article. Not to mention the recent news that ChoicePoint indicates some 145,000 consumers are at risk because of data theft at the company. Furthermore, the latter article notes that California is the only state that by law requires disclosure of such data leaks, and naturally ChoicePoint initially suggested the theft of information might be limited to that state.

So, Congress thinks computer security in the government stinks and only one state in the country requires that citizens be notified of identity theft, and the government wants unchecked authority from congress to store even more information about me using whatever technology they want? My fellow Americans, Congress, and Congressman Tiberi, I too "rise in strong opposition to HR 418."
